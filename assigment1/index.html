Experience Research Project

I believe the purpose of this assignment was to encourage us to engage more critically and thoughtfully with interactive design. Rather than focusing solely on aesthetics or functionality, we were asked to reflect on how an experience feels, how it shapes user behavior, and how it communicates ideas through structure and metaphor.

MyoungAe Kim's portfolio, with its advent calendar-inspired layout, invites exploration in a playful and nonlinear way. This challenges the typical expectations of a digital portfolio and shows how design can be used to guide interaction and create emotional impact.

I think the lecturer wanted us to understand that interactive media isn't just about usability — it's also about storytelling, mood, and user engagement over time. It was also likely intended to inspire us creatively, showing how digital experiences can break away from conventional formats and become more personal, conceptual, and meaningful.

Question 1

1) What was the first thing you paid attention to when interacting with the experience?

The first thing that caught my attention was the grid of numbered boxes on the homepage. It immediately stood out as unusual for a portfolio. Most portfolios use thumbnails, categories, or project names — but here, the numbers were uniform and abstract. This sparked curiosity. The layout reminded me of a calendar or game board, evoking a sense of discovery. I was visually drawn in, trying to guess what could be behind each square, with no immediate clues — just numbers inviting me to interact.

Question 2

2) Spend two minutes with the experience and create a list of each of your discrete actions.

  • Landed on the homepage and scanned the full layout of numbered boxes.
  • Paused to consider what the numbers might represent (dates? order of projects?).
  • Hovered over a few numbers to check for hover animations (subtle change).
  • Clicked on box #1 to see what would happen — a project opened in a smooth animation.
  • Scrolled down within the project to view images, descriptions, and layout.
  • Read through the project narrative and examined the visuals.
  • Clicked a close or back button to return to the homepage.
  • Clicked on #6 — a different project opened with its own distinct tone and aesthetic.
  • Spent time with the layout and read the textual content again.
  • Closed the window, returned to grid — randomly clicked on a higher number like #17.
  • Continued this cycle — opening, reading, closing — noting how each experience was unique.
  • Attempted to navigate through a menu, but there was none — navigation is solely through the calendar.
  • Reflected on how different this interaction model is from traditional web navigation.

Question 3

3) What part of the experience did you spend the most time engaging with?

The individual project panels held my attention the longest. Once a numbered square was clicked, the screen transitioned into a full-page display of a single project. These were rich in content — high-quality images, thoughtful text, and consistent visual styles. I found myself absorbed in reading the designer's process and examining her use of type, color, motion, or interactivity. The contrast between the abstract grid and the rich inner world of each project created a satisfying rhythm that made me want to keep opening more.

×

Question 4

4) What was the most common action in your two-minute interaction with the experience?

Clicking on numbered boxes to reveal projects, then returning to the grid. That pattern — click, discover, explore, return — became the loop of interaction. It felt organic, not forced. There was no need to follow an order. It was like a treasure hunt, discovering a new layer of her portfolio with each interaction.

×

Question 5

5) What is your impression of the intended primary goal of the interactive experience?

The main goal seems to be to create a playful, exploratory space for showcasing design work. MyoungAe Kim likely wants users to engage with her portfolio not just as viewers, but as active participants, navigating intuitively and discovering her work piece by piece. This approach mirrors her design philosophy — one that values interaction, curiosity, and emotional resonance. It's not just about viewing work; it's about experiencing it through micro-surprises and personal engagement.

×

Question 6

6) How does the interactive experience communicate this primary goal?

The interface strips away traditional portfolio conventions — no menus, no categories, no filters. Instead, the numbered boxes are a form of visual metaphor for discovery. There's a gentle gamification at play, where each project becomes a reward for your curiosity. The design also avoids overwhelming the user — by revealing content one piece at a time, the site maintains focus and encourages deeper attention to each project. The quiet animations, white space, and clean typography all reinforce this sense of calm and focused exploration.

×

Question 7

7) What is your impression of how the experience should be interacted with over time? (For how long and how many different times)

It feels designed for multiple, short interactions over time. Like an actual advent calendar, it doesn't pressure you to consume everything at once. You can open a few boxes, take a break, and come back later to continue exploring. This episodic interaction style keeps the experience fresh and sustains user curiosity across sessions. It respects your attention span — rewarding even brief visits — but also encourages returning to explore "what's behind the other numbers."

×

Question 8

8) How does the interactive experience communicate how it should be interacted with over time?

The numbered layout suggests a non-linear, paced approach — you aren't meant to binge it like a scrollable feed. The lack of date labels, titles, or previews reinforces the idea that each interaction is a surprise. The design doesn't enforce urgency or completion — there's no progress tracker or notification system. Instead, it passively invites you back by leaving mysteries unopened. This silent invitation to return is subtle but powerful.

Question 9

9) What other media forms (digital or otherwise) does the experience reference?

  • Advent calendars (obviously) — evoking anticipation and a sense of daily reveal.
  • Puzzle games or discovery-based interfaces, where you explore to learn, not consume.
  • Zines or artistic journals, due to the personal tone and visual storytelling.
  • Possibly installation art, where space, movement, and user flow are part of the message.

These references help frame the site as more than a static portfolio — it feels like an interactive exhibit or experience.

Question 10

10) What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should act when engaging with your research experience?

It tells me I should approach it slowly and curiously, like opening doors in a gallery or flipping pages in a handmade book. There's no pressure to "get through" it — just to enjoy the process of exploration. The advent calendar structure specifically signals that each item deserves its own moment, suggesting a one-at-a-time mindset.

×

Question 11

11) What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should feel when engaging with your research experience?

The experience communicates a feeling of playfulness, wonder, and intimacy. It's designed to make you feel invited, not instructed. There's a personal tone to the work — as if the designer is whispering, "Here's a piece of me. Go ahead, open it." It encourages a sense of wonder and reflection, rather than urgency or information gathering.

×

Question 12

12) What is the most frustrating part of the interaction to you and what makes that part frustrating?

The lack of labels or previews can be a double-edged sword. While it encourages surprise, it also makes revisiting specific projects difficult. If I saw something I liked in box #14 yesterday, I'd have to remember the number or go hunting. There's also no search or overview, so for someone doing in-depth research or needing to quickly locate a project, it might feel inefficient. This limits usability for more goal-oriented tasks.

×

Question 13

13) What is the most satisfying part of the interaction to you and what makes that part satisfying?

The moment of reveal — when you click a box and a project smoothly slides into view — is deeply satisfying. The contrast between the minimal, mysterious homepage and the richly detailed interior projects creates a delightful rhythm. It feels like you're being rewarded for your curiosity. Additionally, each project has its own design personality, so the diversity of experiences adds to the joy of exploration.
